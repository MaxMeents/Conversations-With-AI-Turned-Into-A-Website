1. The Physics Perspective: The Observer Effect and Relativity
In classical physics (Newtonian), the universe is a giant stage that exists independently of us. Space and time are absolute containers, and we are just actors moving through them. However, modern physics complicates this view significantly.

General Relativity (Einstein):
Einstein proved that space and time are not absolute. Your "location" in time is relative to your speed and the gravity acting upon you.

The "Block Universe": Many physicists view spacetime as a "block" where the past, present, and future exist simultaneously. In this view, you aren't "creating" time; you are experiencing a specific slice of it based on your trajectory.
Reference Frames: Every observer has their own "reference frame." While you don't create the universe, your measurement of when and where events happen is unique to you. Two observers moving at different speeds will disagree on the timing of an event, and both are correct.
Quantum Mechanics (The Observer Effect):
This is where your hypothesis gets the most scientific traction.

Wave Function Collapse: In quantum mechanics, particles exist in a state of probability (superposition) until they are measured or observed. The Copenhagen interpretation suggests that observation "collapses" the wave function into a definite state.
Biocentrism: Proposed by Robert Lanza, this theory argues that biology and consciousness are fundamental to the universe, not accidental byproducts. It suggests that space and time are not physical objects but forms of animal sense perception—tools of the mind used to organize information. In this view, yes, you are creating your reality through observation.
However: Mainstream physics generally distinguishes between a "conscious observer" and a "measurement." A particle interacting with a detector collapses the wave function regardless of whether a human brain is involved. Therefore, while observation affects reality, it is unlikely that the human brain alone is generating the entire physical location of the universe.

The Brain is a Dark Room:

Predictive Processing: The brain does not passively record the world like a camera. It actively constructs a simulation. It predicts what should be happening based on past experience and sensory data.

Conclusion from Neuroscience: You are absolutely creating your own experience of location and time. The "you" that exists in a specific moment is a narrative construct of your brain.

3. The Philosophical Perspective: Idealism and Solipsism
Philosophers have debated this for millennia.

Solipsism: This is the idea that only your own mind is sure to exist. In this view, the entire universe, including space, time, and other people, could be a projection of your own mind. While logically possible, it is generally rejected because it is a functional dead-end (it makes scientific inquiry impossible).
Subjective Idealism (George Berkeley): This philosophy argues that "to be is to be perceived" (esse est percipi). Material things do not exist independently of the minds that perceive them.
Kant’s Transcendental Idealism: Immanuel Kant argued that Space and Time are not things "out there" to be discovered. Instead, they are the "irremovable goggles" through which we must view the world. We cannot experience the universe without space and time because our minds are structured to organize data that way.

This is one of the most famous thought experiments in philosophy, often referred to as the "Inverted Spectrum" or "Inverted Qualia" problem.

When you extend this from simple colors to complex objects (like a human being), the question becomes even more fascinating. The short answer is: Yes, it is theoretically possible, but highly unlikely to be radically different due to our shared biology.

Here is a breakdown of how this works, moving from the "Blue vs. Red" problem to the "Seeing a Human Differently" problem.

1. The Problem of "Qualia" (The Internal Experience)
In philosophy, Qualia are individual instances of subjective, conscious experience.

The Data: We can measure the wavelength of light bouncing off a blueberry (about 475 nanometers). This is objective data.
The Experience: We cannot measure "blueness." "Blueness" is just the sensation your brain produces when it receives that 475nm signal.
Because we can never jump inside someone else's consciousness, we can never prove that my internal sensation of "blue" isn't identical to your internal sensation of "red." As long as we both point to the sky and say "Blue," the system works, even if our internal movies are playing different colors.

2. Extending this to Objects (The "Human" Example)
You are asking if this disconnect could apply to shapes and forms. Could I look at a person and see a "human shape," while you look at a person and see what I would call a "floating geometric cube," yet we both call it a "person"?

The Argument for "Yes" (The Simulation Theory):
Your brain does not see the world; it processes electrical signals.

If your brain was wired to interpret the data of "two legs, two arms, one head" as a visual representation of a "purple cloud," you would navigate the world thinking people looked like purple clouds.
However, you would learn to call the top of the cloud the "head" and the bottom wisps the "legs." When you drew a picture of it, your hand would move in a way that you see as a cloud, but I (looking at your drawing) would see as a stick figure.
In this scenario, we would never know we are seeing different things because our outputs (speech, drawing, pointing) would always align.
The Argument for "No" (Evolutionary Biology):
While philosophically possible, science suggests our perceptions are likely very similar. Here is why:

A. Shared Hardware:
Unless one person has a specific neurological condition (like synesthesia or agnosia), we all have roughly the same DNA building our eyes and visual cortex.

The rods and cones in your eyes are arranged in the same patterns as mine.
The edge-detection neurons in your visual cortex (V1 area) fire in the same way when seeing a vertical line.
Because the machinery is identical, the product (the image) is likely very similar. It’s like two TV sets of the same make and model receiving the same broadcast signal; they probably aren't showing two different movies.
B. The Necessity of Structure:
Colors are arbitrary labels for wavelengths. But shapes have physical consequences.

If you saw a human as a "floating cube," you might try to walk through the empty space under the cube. But in reality, there are legs there. You would trip.
Evolution forces our perceptions to map somewhat accurately to physical reality. If your perception of a tiger didn't match the physical shape of the tiger (e.g., where its teeth are), your ancestors would have been eaten.
Therefore, our visual representation of an object must closely resemble its physical structure for us to survive.
3. The "Interface Theory of Perception" (A Middle Ground)
Cognitive scientist Donald Hoffman proposes a theory that bridges these two ideas. He argues that none of us see reality as it is.

He uses the metaphor of a computer desktop:

The Reality: Inside the computer are complex circuits, voltages, and binary code.
The Perception: On your screen, you see a little blue folder icon.
The icon looks nothing like the circuits inside. The icon is a simplification that helps you use the computer.

But an alien, or an AI, or a different species might look at a human and see something entirely different—perhaps the "code" underneath—because they are running a different operating system.
